# Facilitation, Parking Lot, and Decision Log Toolkit

## Meeting design

Every executive or practitioner workshop should end with a clear answer to three questions:

1. What decision was made?
2. Who owns the next step?
3. By when?

## Visible timer pattern

- 10 minutes: objective and current data
- 15 minutes: decisions and blocker removal
- 5 minutes: parking lot review and close

The timer is not cosmetic. It keeps strong opinions from consuming the session.

## Parking lot rules

- park only items that are important but not critical to today’s decision
- assign an owner before the meeting ends
- give every parked item a revisit date

## Decision log fields

- decision statement
- owner
- date
- dependencies
- status
- next review date

## Objection handling pattern

When someone says:

- "The quality is not good enough."
- "This feels unsafe."
- "People are scared of AI."

Do not argue in the abstract. Convert the concern into:

- a hypothesis
- an owner
- a target date
- a measurable success metric

## Example

Concern: "Finance managers do not trust AI-generated summaries."

Experiment:

- hypothesis: approved templates plus manager checklists will hold quality above threshold
- owner: finance controller
- date: April 19, 2026
- success metric: approval stays above 85 percent in the pilot

## Interview line

"My job in a workshop is not to win the debate. My job is to keep the room outcome-focused, surface the real objection, and turn it into a measurable next step."
