# English Technical Interview Q&A

## Q1. How would you identify the right Copilot use cases?

I would start with workflow pain, not with the technology. I look for repetitive drafting, summarization, recap, comparison, or follow-up work where quality can still be reviewed by a human. Then I score the candidate use cases by business value, readiness, risk, and sponsor commitment.

## Q2. How do you measure adoption success?

I separate adoption from value. Adoption includes weekly active usage and repeat usage after training. Value includes time saved, quality approval, support ticket trends, and business-owner validation that the workflow is better than before.

## Q3. How do you handle fear or resistance?

I do not try to win the argument abstractly. I translate the fear into an experiment. For example, if a team is worried about quality, I define a limited pilot, a review standard, an owner, a date, and a success metric.

## Q4. How would you structure training for a large enterprise?

I would not deliver one generic training to everyone. I would build role-based paths for executives, managers, practitioners, and champions. Champions get a train-the-trainer motion, while practitioners get workflow-specific labs and examples.

## Q5. What would you show an executive sponsor?

I would show the business problem, the baseline, the target KPI, the support model, the top risks, and a scale decision recommendation. I would avoid tool-heavy language and keep the readout focused on business impact.

## Q6. How would you design feedback and support for a large rollout?

I would define a tiered model. First, users get self-serve guidance. Second, champions and office hours absorb routine questions. Third, a central adoption helpdesk handles issues that need deeper support. Finally, security or compliance owners review policy-sensitive cases. I would also review feedback themes weekly so support data turns into enablement improvements.

## Q7. How do you keep workshops productive?

I use a visible timer, a parking lot, and a decision log. If a discussion turns into fear or abstract debate, I bring it back to a measurable experiment or an explicit decision.

## Q8. Why did you build this project as a deterministic simulation instead of a live AI demo?

Because I wanted the operating model to be credible and reviewable. For this role, the most important thing is not whether a model can generate text in real time. It is whether the program design, adoption logic, and value measurement hold up under scrutiny.
